Research Finding 1 of 4 · Longitudinal Sentiment Analysis · 2021–2025

The Drift

From 2021 to 2025, negative sentiment in Canadian primary care Reddit discourse climbed from 37.4% to an annual peak of 41.1%. This is the trajectory, month by month — and what it tells us about a system under sustained, growing stress.

Key Findings

Five numbers that define the drift

2021 baseline
37.4%

Average annual negative sentiment in 2021 — already high before the crisis entered public consciousness.

2024 annual peak
41.1%

The worst annual average in the dataset. A +3.7 percentage-point rise from 2021 that compresses four years of policy inaction.

Single worst months

Year-by-Year Analysis

A slow-motion story of a system under pressure

2021

The quiet beginning

With 7,596 total records averaging 633 per month, 2021 is the dataset's baseline — but "baseline" is a relative term. A 37.4% average negativity rate means that even in the year when COVID vaccines were rolling out and systemic optimism peaked, more than one in three primary care conversations expressed frustration. The outlier: April 2021, when negativity surges to 44.2% — a glimpse of what is coming.

37.4% avg neg · 7,596 total records · 633/month avg · Best: Feb at 25.0% · Worst: Apr at 44.2%
2022

The rupture year

Everything changes in 2022. Monthly volume nearly doubles to 951 records on average, and the emotional register shifts sharply: 40.4% negative, up 3.0 points from 2021. July 2022 becomes the dataset's first major spike — 1,577 records in a single month, as pandemic-deferred care collides with accelerating physician retirements and subreddit growth. November 2022 sets a new negativity ceiling at 44.3%. This is the year the conversation stops being fringe and becomes mainstream.

40.4% avg neg · 11,415 total records · 951/month avg · Volume peak: Jul at 1,577 · Sentiment peak: Nov at 44.3%
2023

Resignation becomes the baseline

2023 offers no recovery. At 38.8% average negativity, it sits almost exactly between 2021 and 2022 — but the character of the conversation shifts. Where 2022 posts were shocked and angry, 2023 posts are tired. The monthly range barely varies: from May's relative low of 34.4% to December's 44.1%. Every month of 2023 is more negative than the first month of 2021. Chronic frustration has replaced acute crisis as the dominant tone.

38.8% avg neg · 12,309 total records · 1,025/month avg · Steadiest year — 9.7pp spread across 12 months
2024

The peak — and no one notices

2024 is the worst year in the dataset by every measure. Annual average negativity hits 41.1%. Monthly volume averages 1,446 records, with March reaching an all-time high of 2,284. The spring peak is extraordinary: April and June both hit 44.6% — the highest single-month figures in five years. What is most striking is the absence of a corresponding policy response. There is no announcement that follows the peak, no month where the conversation noticeably brightens. The system breaks a record and the record is not acknowledged.

41.1% avg neg · 17,358 total records · 1,446/month avg · Record month: Mar 2024 (2,284) · Peak neg: Apr & Jun at 44.6%
2025

A cautious dip

January to August 2025 shows the first sustained stretch of lower negativity since mid-2021. At 36.8% average for the partial year, with April (32.6%) and May (32.1%) falling to three-year lows, there is — for the first time — a plausible case for improvement. But the volume remains high (1,085 records/month), and the pattern of alternating high and low months makes a definitive conclusion impossible. The drift may have paused. It has not reversed.

36.8% avg neg · 8,682 records (Jan–Aug) · 1,085/month avg · Lowest recent months: Apr 32.6%, May 32.1%

Interpretation

What the drift tells a health policy researcher

The most important feature of this dataset is not any individual peak — it is the floor. In 2021, the best month (February) saw 25.0% negative sentiment. By 2024, the best month (December) was 36.2%. The floor has risen 11.2 percentage points in four years. This is not a crisis that spikes and recovers; it is a system whose resting state of frustration is continuously worsening.

The volume surge is equally significant. In 2021, 633 Canadians per month were posting about primary care. By 2024, that had grown to 1,446 — not because more people use Reddit, but because more people have something urgent to say. High-volume negative months are self-reinforcing: frustrated posts attract replies, which attract more posts, which makes the problem more visible, which frustrates more people.

The spring 2025 data is the first reason for cautious optimism. Whether the dip in April and May 2025 represents a genuine systemic improvement, a seasonal effect, or simply noise in a dataset that has not yet been fully captured — that is the question this study cannot yet answer. What it can answer is this: as of August 2025, the conversation is still 3.4 percentage points more negative than it was in the first month we measured it.

89,398 posts. Four years. The drift is not a spike — it is a floor that keeps rising.

Every year in this dataset has a worse annual average than the one before it — until 2025, which is trending better but remains above any 2021 level. The question is not whether the system is struggling. The data answered that in January 2021. The question is whether the policies launched in 2022, 2023, and 2024 are actually reaching Canadians — and whether they can see it in the months that remain to be measured.

Method. Sentiment classification using a four-label VADER ML ensemble (Positive / Negative / Neutral / Mixed) applied to 89,398 Reddit records from primary care subreddits across Canada, January 2021 – August 2025. The negative sentiment percentage reported is neg / (pos + neg + neu + mix) per month, excluding records tagged as geo = "Unknown." September 2022 is interpolated from adjacent months (missing from source data). Annual averages weight all months equally; 2025 is partial (January–August only).

Source. Reddit Canadian primary care subreddits · VADER ensemble · n = 89,398 · Memorial University, Faculty of Medicine · 2021–2025.
