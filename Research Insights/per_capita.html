The Hidden Crisis

Raw Reddit post counts make Ontario look like the only place where primary care is broken. Adjust for population and the map reverses: Newfoundland & Labrador is the loudest province per capita, and the Atlantic provinces are systemically under-represented in aggregate policy discourse.

When policy analysts, journalists, and government decision-makers look at raw Reddit data about healthcare, the first thing they see is Ontario — 14,141 records, 15.8% of the total corpus, by far the loudest single voice. It is natural to assume that Ontario's primary care crisis is the primary care crisis. But this conflates signal with population size.

Per-capita normalization divides post counts by each province's 2021 census population and scales to posts per 100,000 residents. The resulting map is not just different — it is nearly inverted.

Ontario has 28.4× more people than Newfoundland and Labrador. But that population advantage completely explains — and then overpowers — its raw post-count advantage. Once you control for the size of the potential "complainant pool," Ontario is an average province. NL is the national leader.

This inversion is not a statistical artifact. NL has experienced some of Canada's most severe family doctor shortages. Approximately 35% of Newfoundlanders lack access to a regular primary care provider. The per-capita Reddit signal is capturing a real geographic pattern in the healthcare crisis.

Anger Map

High per-capita engagement ≠ high negativity

An important nuance: the provinces that post most frequently per capita are not the most negative. Nova Scotia (152/100k) averages 34.9% negative sentiment — well below the corpus mean of 38.8%. NL (164/100k) sits at 35.1% negative. Both Atlantic provinces are engaged but relatively measured in tone.

By contrast, Alberta (92/100k, #4 per capita) has the highest negativity in the dataset at 43.8%. Ontario (95/100k, #3 per capita) is second at 41.2%. The "Big Two" provinces are not just posting a lot — they are posting with more anger per post.

This creates two distinct crisis signatures in the data. Atlantic Canada posts frequently but relatively calmly — their primary care gap is documented, discussed, and accepted as a structural reality. Ontario and Alberta post less frequently (per capita) but with higher emotional intensity — their users may be experiencing a more recent deterioration, or a greater mismatch between expectation and reality.

The Manitoba Anomaly

Manitoba: 1.4 million people, 245 posts — the silent province

Manitoba has 1,395,602 residents and generated only 245 Reddit records across the five-year study period — a rate of just 18 per 100,000 people, by far the lowest of any province with sufficient data. This is not explained by the province having no primary care issues: Manitoba's physician shortage is well-documented, particularly in Winnipeg and rural northern communities.

The most likely explanation is Reddit's demographics. Manitoba skews older than the national average, and Reddit's user base is concentrated in younger, urban, higher-income populations. The low Manitoba signal is a limitation of the data source rather than evidence of satisfaction. Any policy inference that relies on Reddit data must account for this demographic blind spot.

Raw counts are biased. Per-capita rates are policy-relevant.

Ontario's 14,141 records vs. Newfoundland's 855 looks like a 16.5× gap in primary care suffering. It is actually a 28.4× gap in population, with Newfoundlanders posting at 72% higher rates per capita than Ontarians. Policies designed in response to raw-count signals will systematically over-resource already-resourced provinces and leave smaller provinces structurally under-served.

The finding is a methodological argument as much as a substantive one: any analysis of population health that does not normalize for population is not analysis — it is a census.

Method. Per-capita rates calculated as (province_record_count / province_2021_population) × 100,000. Population data from Statistics Canada 2021 Census, Table 98-10-0001-01.
