The Calendar of Care

Is Canadian primary care frustration seasonal? Yes — but not in the way you might expect. There is no month that is "good." There is only a narrow band between the least bad and the consistently worst, and that band barely moves.

Four Seasons

The seasonal pattern, broken down by quarter

Winter (Dec–Feb)
38.5%

Relatively quiet

December through February sees the lowest average negativity of any season. Holiday-period posts may skew toward sharing and support. February is the best single month in the full dataset at 38.3%.

Spring (Mar–May)
39.3%

Rising pressure

March and April see a steady increase as the post-holiday backlog becomes visible. May dips slightly — but April 2024 (44.6%) was the worst single month in the entire dataset, a spring that stood apart.

Summer (Jun–Aug)
39.3%

Coverage gaps

Summer frustration is steady. Walk-in clinic posts increase as family doctors take vacation. Locum coverage gaps are a common theme. July has produced the highest-volume individual months — 1,577 records in July 2022 alone.

Fall (Sep–Nov) ▲ WORST
40.5%

The hardest quarter

September through November is consistently the most negative quarter in every year of data. Flu season loads a system that cannot absorb additional demand. November (41.3%) has been the worst month in four of the five years measured.

Interpretation

Why the seasonal pattern matters for policy

The 3.3-percentage-point spread between the best month (May, 38.0%) and the worst (November, 41.3%) tells a story that is simultaneously reassuring and alarming. Reassuring, because the system does not catastrophically deteriorate in any single month — there is no true "crisis spike" that policy could easily target with a one-time intervention. Alarming, for exactly the same reason: the frustration is not seasonal in nature. It is structural.

If the negativity were purely seasonal — driven by flu outbreaks or holiday closures — we would expect January and December to be the worst months, not November, and we would expect summer to be dramatically better. Instead, the data shows that summer is essentially indistinguishable from fall, and that fall's additional burden appears to be the accumulated weight of a year of under-met demand rather than any specific seasonal trigger.

The fall peak is consistent across years. In 2021, November was 39.5%. In 2022, November was 44.3%. In 2023, November was 40.2%. In 2024, November was 39.9%. Four consecutive years in which November is among the highest-negativity months. This is not noise — it is a repeating structural pattern that should be a known, plannable event in provincial health system calendars.

September's relatively low volume (716 records/year average, roughly half of most months) despite its high negativity (40.5%) may reflect that people returning from summer are still adjusting to the system — the complaints spike in volume by October, but the emotional register is already elevated the moment the new season begins.

There is no good time of year to need a family doctor in Canada.

The 3.3-percentage-point range from best month to worst is a system telling you that its problems are not seasonal — they are architectural. November is the peak not because November is uniquely hard, but because the rest of the year's unmet need has accumulated into it. Policy interventions that target specific seasons will find no clean on-ramp. The frustration is always there. It is just slightly more visible in the fall.

Method. Seasonal averages computed by aggregating all records (excluding Unknown geo) by calendar month across 2021–2025, then computing neg / (pos + neg + neu + mix) per month-of-year. Volume per year average = total records in that month-of-year / number of years with data. 2025 data is partial (January–August); September–December 2025 are not included in averages for those months.
