The Calendar of Care

Is Canadian primary care frustration seasonal? Yes — but not in the way you might expect. There is no month that is "good." There is only a narrow band between the least bad and the consistently worst, and that band barely moves.

The relative quiet

Lowest average negativity of any quarter. February (38.3%) is the best single month in the full dataset. December and January are slightly more negative — but all three are teal nodes in the helix.

Rising pressure

March and April escalate steadily. May dips slightly — but April 2024 (44.6%) was the worst single month in the entire dataset, a spring anomaly that shattered the seasonal pattern.

Coverage gaps

The Anomaly

2024 spring broke the seasonal pattern — and that is significant

The seasonal model predicts that spring should be a relatively moderate quarter — lower than fall, roughly comparable to summer. April 2024 (44.6%) and June 2024 (44.6%) violated this prediction dramatically. These are the two worst months in the entire dataset, and they are in spring and early summer — months that the seasonal model would not have predicted as peaks.

This anomaly suggests that in 2024, a structural driver was strong enough to override the seasonal pattern. The most likely explanation is that the accumulation of unresolved primary care access problems reached a point where even the "good" seasons stopped being good. When the structural driver dominates the seasonal one, it is a signal that the system has moved to a different regime — one where seasonal amelioration is no longer reliably available as a pressure valve.

Policy interventions designed around the seasonal calendar (e.g., increasing locum coverage in fall, staffing walk-in clinics for the winter surge) may be insufficient if the structural driver is strong enough to produce peak-level negativity in non-peak months. The 2024 spring anomaly is an argument for structural reform, not seasonal management.

There is no good time of year to need a family doctor in Canada.

The 3.3-percentage-point range from best month to worst is a system telling you that its problems are not seasonal — they are architectural. November peaks not because November is uniquely hard, but because the rest of the year's unmet need has accumulated into it. Policy that targets specific seasons will find no clean on-ramp. The frustration is always there. It is just slightly more visible in the fall.
