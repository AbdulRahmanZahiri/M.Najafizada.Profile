This gap is not a quirk of word choice — it reflects a fundamental divergence in how Canadians relate to the primary care system. "Waitlist" is the vocabulary of lived experience: specific, personal, frustrated. "Primary Care" is the vocabulary of abstraction: systemic, political, hopeful. The same healthcare crisis, seen from two emotional altitudes.

The Dominant Term

"Family Doctor" appears in 37,969 records — 42% of the entire corpus

No other term comes close. "Family Doctor" is mentioned in 42.5% of all 89,398 records analysed — making it the defining vocabulary of Canadian primary care discourse. When Canadians go online to talk about healthcare access, the phrase they reach for most often is not "primary care system" or "physician shortage." It is the personal: my family doctor.

That personalism matters. "Family Doctor" carries a +0.096 average sentiment — not negative in aggregate, but with 38.3% negative posts. This is a term associated with both the care Canadians value (the positive 24.6%) and the crisis they experience when it is unavailable (the negative 38.3%). It is the pivot point of the entire discourse.

The Solution Signal

Nurse Practitioner: the most solution-oriented keyword in the dataset

With only 3,046 mentions (3.4% of the corpus), "Nurse Practitioner" is a small but meaningful keyword — and one of the most positive. At +0.259 average compound sentiment and 29.3% positive vs. only 23.6% negative, it is one of three keywords where positive posts outnumber negative ones.

This is not accidental. When Canadians mention Nurse Practitioners, they are typically doing one of two things: celebrating a positive care experience ("my NP is wonderful"), or advocating for expanded NP scope as a policy solution ("NPs should be able to prescribe"). Both uses trend positive. It suggests that healthcare alternatives — when people actually encounter them — carry hope rather than frustration.

The contrast with "Waitlist" (65.9% negative) is stark: Canadians discussing what is blocking their access express despair; Canadians discussing what might improve access express cautious optimism. This is the emotional map of a system that is failing not because people have given up, but because the path forward remains unclear.

Two words. One problem. One compound point of distance between them.

"Waitlist" (−0.411) and "Primary Care" (+0.482) describe the same healthcare system — one from inside the waiting room, one from outside it. The 0.893-point gap between them is not just a statistical curiosity. It is the emotional architecture of a healthcare crisis: the people most affected are the most frustrated, and the people discussing solutions are those who have not yet had to wait.
