Two Voices

Canadians reach for different words depending on whether they are searching for care or describing it from the outside. Those words carry measurably different emotional signatures — and the gap between the most negative and most positive keyword is 0.893 compound sentiment points wide.

The 0.893 gap is not a quirk of word choice — it reflects a structural divergence in how Canadians relate to their primary care system. "Waitlist" is the vocabulary of lived experience: specific, personal, frustrated. "Primary Care" is the vocabulary of abstraction: systemic, political, hopeful. The Mixed Voice

32% mixed sentiment in "Waitlist" — the highest ambivalence in the dataset

The VADER classifier assigns four labels: Positive, Negative, Neutral, and Mixed. Mixed captures posts where the emotional signal is conflicted — neither venting nor celebrating, but holding both simultaneously. "Waitlist" has the highest mixed-sentiment rate of any keyword at 32.0%.

This is psychologically and politically distinct from pure negativity. The emotional signature of a Waitlist post is not rage — it is resigned frustration: "I know this is wrong, I've accepted it anyway, and I'm still here." Resigned acceptance is harder to mobilise into advocacy than acute anger. It is the sentiment of a population that has stopped expecting change.

The 37,969 Anchor

"Family Doctor" — 42% of the corpus

"Family Doctor" appears in 37,969 records — 42.5% of the entire dataset. No other keyword comes close. When Canadians write about primary care, the phrase they reach for most is not "primary care system" or "physician shortage." It is the personal: my family doctor. Or, increasingly: I don't have a family doctor.

Its +0.096 average and 38.3% negativity reflect its universality. Family Doctor is used for everything: praising a good doctor, mourning a retired one, seeking advice on finding one. Its emotional range is the widest precisely because its usage is the broadest. But the 38.3% negativity baseline — in a keyword that should encompass many positive interactions — is a quiet signal of how often those interactions are strained.

The Solution Signal

Nurse Practitioner: where the hopeful data lives

With +0.259 average sentiment and 29.3% positive vs. 23.6% negative, "Nurse Practitioner" is one of three keywords where positive posts outnumber negative ones. At 3,046 mentions it is small — but it is the only high-volume keyword in the dataset where people discussing an alternative access model report a measurably positive experience.

This has a direct policy implication: expanded NP scope and independent NP practices are not just theoretically appealing — they are already generating positive sentiment in the people who have encountered them. The 0.259 compound score is a signal that real-world NP access is working in ways that justify further investment.

Two words. One system. 0.893 points apart.

"Waitlist" (−0.411) and "Primary Care" (+0.482) describe the same healthcare crisis — one from inside the waiting room, one from the policy room. The gap is the distance between the person waiting and the person proposing. Closing that gap requires more than language — it requires policy that makes the waiting room feel as hopeful as the proposal room.
