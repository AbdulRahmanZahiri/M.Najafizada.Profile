Everything, except

Lucienne is built from the 7,161 posts about what primary care covers. She has a family doctor — she is the lucky half. Her year is about discovering the edges of the word "comprehensive."

Find Lucienne in the crowd. Every cost, wait, and closed door in her story is drawn from the real posts — nothing is invented.
Who is Lucienne?

Lucienne is a composite — one person assembled from the thousands who wrote about the range of care: what a family doctor handles, what a pharmacist rescues, what needs a referral, and what was never insured at all. Her year is a braid of their years. She is fiction in name only. The numbers are the data's.

Day 0the lucky half

The annual physical

Lucienne has what half the country is searching for: a family doctor. The annual check-up is the system at its best — blood work, blood pressure, the screening questions. For everything this visit covers, primary care works exactly as promised.

From the data · preventive care & screening is the headline of 9.2% of this pillar's posts and touches 14.4% — the part of "comprehensive" that mostly delivers.
Week 3the first edge

She asks about her anxiety

At the end of the visit, Lucienne mentions the anxiety. Her doctor listens, and can prescribe — that much is covered. But therapy? People in the posts spell out what Lucienne learns now: seeing a psychologist or therapist in private practice isn't covered by the provincial plan. A psychiatrist is covered, but the referral is, as one commenter puts it, generally for prescriptions.

From the data · mental health is this pillar's largest theme — 34.4% of all 7,161 posts. Therapy sessions are cited around $100–$200, some $400+, and described as not covered by provincial plans.
Month 2the price of a diagnosis

The ADHD question

Lucienne has wondered about ADHD for years. Her doctor won't prescribe a controlled medication without a proper diagnosis from a psychiatrist — a rule the posts repeat often. The public route is a long referral queue. The private route is an assessment with a four-figure price tag. Lucienne puts it off. The posts are full of people putting it off.

From the data · private assessments are cited at $2,000–$5,000. The most-cited waits in mental-health and referral contexts: 6 months (172×) and 3 months (125×).
Month 4a condition for life

The diagnosis: type 2 diabetes

The blood work comes back. Now Lucienne is a chronic patient — the kind of patient, the posts note bitterly, some doctors quietly avoid taking on because complex care is unpaid work. Her own doctor manages the basics. For the rest, she's pointed somewhere unexpected: the pharmacy, where some chains keep a diabetes educator on staff.

From the data · chronic conditions run through 11.6% of this pillar. Posts describe patients with diabetes being declined by physicians, and pharmacies staffing diabetes teaching to fill the gap.
Month 6the quiet rescue

The pharmacist steps in

Lucienne's prescription runs out while her doctor's office is booked solid. The fix comes from across the counter: the pharmacist issues an emergency refill to bridge her to her appointment — a power the posts describe pharmacists using constantly, along with vaccinations and, in some provinces, prescribing for minor conditions. Nobody planned the pharmacist as a pillar of primary care. The data says they've become one.

From the data · pharmacist-delivered care is the second-largest theme: 12.1% of posts as the main subject, touched by 19.6% — emergency refills, renewals, vaccines, minor ailments.
Month 8outside the circle

The tooth, and the eyes

A cracked molar, and a prescription for glasses. Lucienne discovers the oldest edge of all: dental and vision were never inside Medicare to begin with. No referral, no waitlist — just a bill. The posts say the quiet part out loud: make mental health, dental, vision, and hearing part of the provincial plans. Until then, the body Canada covers ends at the mouth and the eyes.

From the data · dental, vision & allied care appear in 12.0% of posts — overwhelmingly as things not insured; repeated calls to add dental, vision, hearing, mental health to provincial coverage.
Year 1the map of her care

One patient, six addresses

By year's end, Lucienne's "comprehensive" care has a map: the doctor's office for the body, the pharmacy for the bridge, a psychiatrist's waitlist for the mind, a private therapist she pays for when she can, a dentist and an optometrist she pays for always. She has a family doctor — and still, most weeks, her health care happens somewhere else.

From the data · the word is "comprehensive"; the pattern across 7,161 posts is a patchwork — care split by what's covered, stopgapped, or excluded.
And then her story splits — the way the data does
The rare win

The referral finally lands

Months later, the psychiatrist consult comes through — the diagnosis, and coverage for the medication that follows. A nurse practitioner or team clinic bundles more of her care under one roof. The circle, for Lucienne, widens.

From the data: psychiatrists are covered once reached; the most-cited referral waits are 3–6 months.
The common reality

The patchwork holds

For most, the edges stay where they are. Therapy stays out of pocket or out of reach; the assessment stays unaffordable; the teeth wait for a crisis. People cite waits stretching years — and budgets that decide what "health" includes.

From the data: waits cited in this pillar run to 2 years (173×) and 5 years (107×); therapy at $100–$200+/session, assessments $2,000–$5,000.

Lucienne has a doctor. She still doesn't have comprehensive care.

That is this pillar's finding, assembled from 7,161 posts: coverage draws a circle around the body — and leaves the mind to a referral wall, the mouth and the eyes to the market, and the gaps in between to the pharmacist. Getting in the door, it turns out, is only the first pillar. The second is what's actually behind it.

The posts don't ask for luxury. They ask, again and again, for the same short list: mental health, dental, vision, hearing — inside the plan.
