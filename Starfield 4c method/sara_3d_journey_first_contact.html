A research composite · one person, built from the data

The wait

Out of the 77,241 in that crowd, one person. Sara isn't real — she's a single character built from thousands of real journeys in the dataset. This is her year trying to find a family doctor.

Find Sara in the crowd. Every wait-time and dead end in her story below is drawn from the real posts — nothing is invented.
Who is Sara?

Sara is a composite — one person assembled from the thousands who wrote about reaching primary care. Her timeline is a braid of their timelines: the calls, the lists, the waits, the workarounds, each measured by what people actually reported. She is fiction in name only. The numbers are the data's.

Day 0the door she didn't know was closing

Her doctor retires

It starts quietly. Sara's family doctor retires — or she moves to a new province and finds she's at the back of a fresh queue. Overnight, with no warning, she has no one. She isn't worried yet. She assumes she'll just sign up somewhere.

From the data · losing a doctor to a retirement or move begins 3.1%–3.7% of access posts; many also mention a $25–$100 fee just to transfer their own file.
Week 1~10 clinics called

The phone calls begin

She makes a list of every clinic in town and starts dialling. The answer is the same at each one, almost word for word: not accepting new patients. A receptionist — kindly, wearily — tells her there's no point calling around; the doctors simply aren't taking anyone.

From the data · "not accepting new patients" is the near-universal reply across the 42,225 search posts; people report calling for weeks.
Week 2added to the list — no date given

811, and the waitlist

Someone tells her to call the provincial health line. She does, and is added to the official wait list — Health Care Connect, or its local equivalent. No timeline. No number. Just a confirmation that she is, now, officially waiting.

From the data · the registry is the formal route in the 6,067 waitlist posts — and it comes with no timeline, only the list.
Month 112-hour wait

The first walk-in

A sinus infection won't quit, so she tries a walk-in clinic. She arrives early and waits most of the day — twelve hours by the time she's seen, by a doctor she's never met and won't see again. Nothing is added to a record, because there is no record. It works, barely. It is not care.

From the data · 12-hour walk-in and ER waits are among the most-cited durations, reported hundreds of times.
Month 2paying to be seen

Going private, just for a refill

She needs a prescription renewed and can't face another all-day wait. So she does what a fifth of these posts describe: she pays. A virtual service connects her to a doctor in minutes — some are covered, some charge a fee — and she starts to wonder, quietly, whether this is just how it works now.

From the data · paying, private clinics, and virtual visits are mentioned in 21.7% of access posts — the single most common coping theme.
Month 46-hour ER wait

The emergency room, for something it isn't for

One night the chest pain is frightening enough that she goes to the ER — not because it belongs there, but because there is nowhere else. Six hours in a plastic chair. It turns out to be something a family doctor would have caught months earlier, at a desk, in ten minutes.

From the data · the ER is the fallback in 11.9% of posts; 6-hour waits for non-emergencies are repeatedly described.
Month 6the list hasn't moved

Half a year, no movement

Six months in, her place on the list is exactly where it started. She begins reading other people's stories and finds the timelines that wait for her: two years is common, five isn't rare, and somewhere she sees the words ten-year wait list. In one province, people are told four years — today.

From the data · waitlist durations cited most often: 2 years (885×), 5 years (562×), 6 months (628×) — and reports of 10-year lists.
Year 1still calling, weekly

A full year, and a door behind the door

By a year, calling clinics has become a habit. And a new wall appears: she needs a referral she can't get. A psychiatrist, a specialist — both require a family doctor to send her, and she has none. The system that won't let her in the front door has quietly locked every room behind it too.

From the data · mental-health access (mentioned in 8.7%) and specialists both need a GP referral; ADHD assessments cost $700+ privately or wait months.
And then her story splits — the way the data does
The rare win · ~4–24 months

Someone finally says yes

The weekly calls pay off — a clinic opens a slot — or a nurse practitioner at a community Access clinic takes her on. She never sees a family doctor, exactly. But she has someone now, and she keeps them for years.

From the data: persistence "paid off after 4 months"; one person, assigned an NP, stayed with them 9 years.
The common reality · year 2 and on

She is still on the list

For most, year one becomes year two, then three. The walk-ins and the paid visits become permanent. Some, after long enough, simply stop trying — building a smaller, lonelier version of care because the real thing never came.

From the data: 2 years is the most-cited wait; some report 8 years on the list before giving up.

Sara is not one person. She is the average of 77,241.

Her year is a composite, but it is not a guess. Every wait she endures, every door that closes, every workaround she improvises is drawn from what real people wrote — sequenced into a single timeline so the shape of the ordeal becomes visible. Read alone, each post is one frustration. Read together, as one journey, they are a year of someone's life spent simply trying to get in.

And that is the finding beneath all of it: for most people, primary care in Canada isn't about the medicine at all. It's about the wait at the door.

Method. "Sara" is a composite persona. Her timeline is assembled from the 77,241 posts and comments classified as Contact/Access (Starfield's 4C framework). Each stage and duration reflects patterns and figures drawn from the data — durations are the most frequently reported values for that stage; situations are paraphrased from real posts. No detail is invented and no individual is identified.

Source. Reddit, Canadian primary-care discourse, January 2021 – August 2025 · n = 89,398 (Contact/Access = 77,241) · Memorial University, Faculty of Medicine.
