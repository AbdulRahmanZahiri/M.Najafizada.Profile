The golden thread

Margaret is built from the 723 posts about the relationship itself — the doctor who knows you, kept for decades, lost in a single letter. Hers is the pillar people only mention once it's gone.

Find Margaret in the crowd. Every year, fee, and rupture in her story is drawn from the real posts — nothing is invented.
Who is Margaret?

Margaret is a composite — one person assembled from the hundreds who wrote about keeping a doctor: the decades-long relationships, the retirement letters that end them, the fees charged for the file left behind. Her quarter-century is a braid of theirs. She is fiction in name only. The numbers are the data's.

25 years agothe thread begins

Her first appointment

Margaret's doctor takes her on when taking a new patient is still an ordinary thing. Over the years he learns her the slow way — the pregnancies, the blood pressure, the winter she didn't sleep. By the end he doesn't read her chart. He wrote it.

From the data · posters cite relationships of 10 years (6×), 25 years (4×), 15 years (4×), 13 years — decades with one doctor, described in passing, like weather.
Day 0the snap

The retirement letter

It arrives without warning: her doctor is retiring. The posts describe this exact moment again and again — some retiring doctors leave instructions on where to go next and how to transfer the file; many, the posts note with an edge, retire with no replacement set up at all. Twenty-five years of being known ends in one page.

From the data · a doctor retiring or leaving is this pillar's largest specific theme — 42 posts (5.8%) — and the rupture that starts most continuity stories.
Week 2the invoice

A bill for her own memory

To take her history anywhere, Margaret must buy it. The posts put hard numbers on this: $125 per person for one household's records after their doctor retired; a parent asked for $200+ for her own and her son's files — and unable to afford it, wondering for years if not paying was a mistake. One commenter explains the other side: retiring doctors must pay to store charts for a decade, and the fee covers it. Reasonable, maybe. It still means the record of your life has a price.

From the data · record-transfer fees cited: $30, $125 per person, $200+ — set against a 10-year storage obligation the fee helps cover.
Month 3strangers with her chart

Twenty-five years in ten minutes

Her care now happens at walk-ins — a different clinician each visit, none of whom will see her twice. Each time, she compresses a quarter century into the first ten minutes: the allergies, the surgeries, the medication that didn't work in 2011. The PDF she paid for sits in her bag. The knowing is gone; what's left is the retelling.

From the data · walk-in care threads through 5.8% of this pillar — care without memory, the structural opposite of continuity.
Year 1back at pillar one

The registry

Margaret joins the provincial wait list for a new family doctor — and lands in the world of the first pillar, alongside the tens of thousands searching. The posts are blunt about her odds: have you ever had to find a new doctor? It could be years. Continuity, once lost, waits at the back of the longest queue in the system.

From the data · paraphrasing a real reply: finding a new doctor "could be years." Switching or finding anew appears in 3.0% of this pillar's posts.
Year 3the void

The years without

Time passes the way the posts say it does. One commenter — who worked for a health authority, no less — describes not seeing a doctor in ten years: not happy about it, nothing to be done about it, bleak. Margaret's thread doesn't reconnect. It just gets older, along with her, and the chart no one is keeping anymore.

From the data · paraphrased from a real post: "haven't seen a doctor in 10 years… I worked for the health authority and it's just bleak."

The rare win

A new thread, from page one

Years later, a clinic finally says yes — a new family doctor or nurse practitioner takes her on, removes her from the old roster, and starts a fresh chart. The knowing begins again from zero: slower, later, but real. Some posters got here. It took years.

From the data: getting accepted removes you from the previous roster — the rebuild is possible, just slow.
The common reality

She becomes her own archive

For most, the thread stays cut. Margaret keeps the folder herself — the paid-for PDF, the medication list, the retellings — because the only person who still holds her whole history is her. Continuity survives as a document, carried by its subject.

From the data: the void is real — a decade without a doctor, described first-hand; the file outlives the relationship.

In 89,398 posts, no one ever started a thread about continuity.

That is this pillar's strangest, saddest finding. All 723 continuity posts are replies — zero are original posts. Nobody asks the internet for someone who knows them, because that isn't something you can search for, wait-list, or walk into. It only becomes visible at the moment it ends: a retirement letter, a records invoice, a stranger asking you to start from the beginning.

Continuity was never in the building. It was in one person's memory of you — and in this system, memory retires too.

Method. "Margaret" is a composite persona. Her story is assembled from the 723 posts classified as Continuity under Starfield's 4C framework — 0.8% of the 89,398-post corpus, and notably 100% comments (zero original posts). Specific themes are small and diffuse: doctor retiring/leaving 5.8%, walk-in discontinuity 5.8%, long-term relationships 3.5%, records & transfer 3.0%; the remainder is general care discussion in which continuity surfaces incidentally. Years, fees ($30, $125, $200+), and situations are paraphrased from real posts. No detail is invented and no individual is identified.
