A research composite · pillar 3 of 4 · coordination

The space between

Mathieu is built from the 2,735 posts about care that has to travel — between a family doctor, a lab, an imaging clinic, and a specialist. He has a doctor. He has a problem. The maze is everything in between.

Find Mathieu in the crowd. Every dead end and every wait in his story is drawn from the real posts — nothing is invented.
Who is Mathieu?

Mathieu is a composite — one person assembled from the thousands who wrote about referrals, requisitions, results, and records: the connections between providers, and how often they fail. His journey is a braid of theirs. He is fiction in name only. The numbers are the data's.

Day 0the handoff begins

The referral goes out

Mathieu's elbow has been wrong for months, and his family doctor agrees: this needs a specialist. In Canada that means a referral — the doctor sends it, and Mathieu's job, he's told, is to wait for a call. The single largest theme in this pillar begins exactly here: a piece of paper leaving one office for another.

From the data · specialist referrals are the largest theme — 39.4% of all 2,735 coordination posts. Waiting runs through 43.4%.
Week 6the first dead end

Silence, then the phone call

Six weeks, no call. Mathieu phones the specialist's office himself and hears what the posts describe again and again: nothing ever arrived — or nothing was ever sent — and either way, nobody was going to tell him. He learns the first law of the maze: no one calls you when a handoff fails.

From the data · posts describe referrals and results that were never received, never sent, never heard back about — and short waits of 2–3 weeks (cited 69× and 56×) quietly becoming months.
Month 2a new piece of paper

The requisition

The referral is re-sent, and now a second document enters the maze: a requisition for an MRI. Tests, labs, and imaging are the second-biggest thread of this pillar — every scan its own queue, every result its own journey back. Mathieu is now waiting on two pieces of paper instead of one.

From the data · tests, labs & results run through 30.5% of posts. The most-cited waits: 6 months (138×), 3 months (81×), 2 months (71×).
Month 6the results go nowhere

He looks up his own scan

The MRI happens. Then — nothing. One poster describes exactly this: the results didn't reach the doctor in time, so they gave up waiting and looked them up themselves through the provincial portal. Mathieu does the same, reading radiology language alone at his kitchen table. The receptionist's comfort is the maze's second law: if it were bad, someone would have called.

From the data · dozens of posts describe chasing results yourself; portals and e-records appear in 10.7% — often as the only way to see your own scan.
Month 8the six-month rule

The referral expires

When the specialist's office finally has an opening, Mathieu learns the maze's strangest rule from the posts: see the specialist again within six months, or the referral lapses and you start over with a new one. One long-time patient calls it what it is — such a waste — a door that quietly relocks itself while you stand in line for it.

From the data · paraphrasing a real post: seeing the same specialist for years still means a fresh referral if more than six months pass between visits.
Month 18the private detour

He pays his way out of one corridor

This beat belongs to one poster almost verbatim, paraphrased: eighteen months waiting for an MRI, never hearing back from the public option, and finally paying for a private scan — because only then could the elbow surgery move forward. The maze has an exit you can buy. Not everyone can.

From the data · 18 months (37×), 2 years (130×), 3 years (78×) and 5 years (78×) are the long waits people cite around referrals, scans, and surgery.
Year 2back to the start

The specialist sends him home

The consult finally happens — and ends the way the maze always ends: follow up with your family doctor. The findings travel back the same fragile paths they came by. Mathieu returns to the start square carrying his own file, because by now he's learned the maze's final law: the only courier who never loses the papers is the patient.

From the data · follow-up & handoffs appear in 6.2% of posts, records transfer in 5.4% — the return trip is part of the maze too.
And then his story splits — the way the data does
The rare win

The system that talks to itself

Some posters found the exits: a lab portal where results appear in a day, visible to you and your doctor; a provincial records app with everything at any clinician's fingertips; a nurse practitioner who requisitions, prescribes, and refers without a single dropped baton.

From the data: portal & e-record posts (10.7%) are this pillar's most hopeful — "10/10 recommend" energy, paraphrased.
The common reality

The patient is the courier

For most, coordination stays a job the sick do themselves: phoning to confirm the fax landed, chasing results, restarting lapsed referrals, hand-carrying history between offices that don't speak. The connections exist — the patient is what holds them together.

From the data: waits cited to 2 years (130×) and 5 years (78×); dozens describe doing the follow-up themselves.

The offices don't talk to each other. So the patient becomes the messenger.

That is this pillar's finding, assembled from 2,735 posts: the referral is the front door to everything — specialists, scans, surgery — and the corridors between offices are where care goes quiet. Papers vanish without notice. Results reach the patient before the doctor, or reach no one. Referrals expire while their owners wait. Coordination, in this archive, is not something the system does. It's something the sick do.

And the fix the posts keep pointing to is almost boring: let the records travel — portals, shared files, a system that talks to itself.
